Key differentiators vs. the market

Breach remediation outside the cap (Cl. 8.3)

Workday contractually pays for forensic investigation, notifications, credit monitoring (1yr), and call center (1yr) when Workday causes a breach — not capped. No peer vendor does this.

48-hour breach notification — contractual, not aspirational

Cl. 5.3 requires Workday to notify within 48 hours of becoming aware of a Security Breach. Snowflake commits to 72 hours but took 45 days in 2024. GCP and OpenAI use undefined "without undue delay."

24-month liability cap for confidentiality and security breaches

Enhanced cap of 24 months of fees (vs. 12-month standard) for breach of confidentiality, security, or privacy. Most competitors are 12 months. Breach remediation costs are on top of this — excluded from the cap entirely.

Customer owns all Outputs — explicit in the contract

The Universal Amendment explicitly states Customer owns all right, title, and interest in AI Outputs made available in the Customer's instance. Contract-level commitment — not embedded in a policy document or tier-dependent.

Single agreement for every Workday product

One Order Form + MSA + Security Exhibit + DPE + Product Terms covers ERP, HCM, Finance, Sana, and every future product. Competitors use layered URL-incorporated stacks where continued use can equal acceptance of changes.

Product Terms: 30-day notice, no material decrease in protections

Workday cannot materially decrease security, privacy, or SLA protections through Product Terms updates. Any change requires 30 days' notice before it takes effect. Most competitors have no equivalent guarantee.

The Two Migration Pathways
Two different documents — same end state. Know which one your customer is on before the conversation.
The key question to ask first: Does the customer have an existing Workday MSA? If yes → Amendment path (Level 2/3). If no, or their MSA is being fully replaced → Full UMSA path (Level 1).
Level 2 & 3

Universal Amendment to the MSA

For existing customers — amends their current MSA
Who it applies to
Existing Workday customers with a current MSA. The amendment layers on top of the existing agreement.
What they sign
Universal Amendment to the MSA. Takes effect at the start date of the Order Form that incorporates it. The existing MSA remains in full force except where specifically amended.
What changes
  • Product addenda replaced by Product Terms
  • Enhanced Features framework — explicit AI/ML feature lists and in-tenant data contribution controls
  • AI Output ownership explicitly stated
  • 30-day notice required for Product Terms changes
  • No material decrease in security/privacy/SLA allowed
What stays the same
Existing MSA remains the core agreement. Pricing, SLAs, and commercial protections carry forward. This is not a re-trade.
Key talking point
"We're not replacing your agreement — we're adding a layer that gives you access to all of Workday's AI innovations without ever signing another addendum."
Common objection
"Why do we need to sign anything new?" — Without the Amendment, the customer cannot access Flex Credits, Sana, or any new AI SKUs. Frame as unlocking, not obligating.
Level 1

Universal Main Subscription Agreement

For new customers or full MSA replacements
Who it applies to
New Workday customers, or existing customers whose MSA is being fully superseded and replaced.
What they sign
Universal Main Subscription Agreement (UMSA). Full replacement — supersedes and replaces the existing MSA. Takes effect on the Effective Date or the date on the Order Form referencing UMSA terms.
What changes
  • Existing MSA is superseded and replaced in full
  • New Universal Security Exhibit (USE) and Universal Data Processing Exhibit (UDPE) apply
  • Product Terms replace all prior product addenda
  • Enhanced liability cap: 24-month for confidentiality/security/privacy breach
  • Breach remediation costs outside the cap (Cl. 8.3)
What stays the same
Commercial terms, pricing, and SLA commitments from the Order Form carry forward. Migration is a structural change — not a commercial renegotiation.
Key talking point
"You're on the same contractual foundation as every new Workday customer — with all of our premium security and AI protections built in from day one."
Common objection
"We're replacing our entire MSA — can we redline it?" — The UMSA is a platform standard. Same terms apply to every customer. There is no bespoke negotiation on base terms. This is actually faster for Legal to review, not slower.

Objection Prep Guide
The 6 recurring objection clusters — what they really mean and how to respond
The pattern behind every objection: Nearly every uMSA objection is a variation of one question — "If we migrate to access Workday's AI, what happens to our data, our outputs, our control, and our risk?" The answer is always: you're better protected under the uMSA than you were before.
Tier 1 — Most common · Address proactively
"Workday is going to train on our data." ▼
What they're really worried about
They've seen news about AI companies training on customer data. They assume Workday operates the same way. Often triggered by the word "AI" appearing in the new contract framework.
Approved positioning
Workday does not train external AI or ML models on Customer Content. It's in the contract. MSA Cl. 3(a): Customer Content is used only to provide the Service. The Universal Amendment adds an explicit statement confirming this. This is a contractual obligation — not a policy document that can be updated quietly.

Important distinction: "data contribution" is separate from training. Data contribution refers to opt-in/opt-out of using anonymized, aggregated signals to improve Workday models. The customer controls this toggle in-tenant. Training on identifiable Customer Content is prohibited, period.
When to escalate
If the customer wants to add custom AI data use language beyond what the contract provides, loop in Legal. Bespoke AI addenda are not on the table, but there are standard paths for transparency requests.
Resource to send
AI Fact Sheet · Universal Amendment (AI Systems section) · Product Terms for applicable product
"We want our own AI addendum / we need more AI-specific contract language." ▼
What they're really worried about
Legal has seen AI-specific addenda from other vendors and assumes the uMSA is missing protections because there's no standalone AI section. They may not realize the protections are distributed across the contract architecture.
Approved positioning
The AI protections are already in the contract — just distributed across the right sections. Walk them through it: Cl. 3(a) covers no training. The Universal Amendment covers Output ownership and AI System governance. Product Terms cover Enhanced Features and AI controls. AI Fact Sheets provide transparency on model training, inputs/outputs, and limitations.

Workday cannot agree to bespoke AI terms for individual customers. The Product Terms are a platform standard — offering a custom addendum would create inconsistent protections and is not the model.
When to escalate
If the customer pushes hard on custom AI language, loop in Legal for a read on what is and isn't possible. Do not make commitments on bespoke AI terms without Legal's involvement.
Resource to send
AI Fact Sheet · Universal Amendment (highlight AI Systems section) · Product Terms · Responsible AI FAQ
"Who owns the AI outputs? What exactly counts as an 'Output'?" ▼
What they're really worried about
Legal teams know AI output ownership is unsettled territory. They want to know exactly what they own vs. what Workday retains. Often they've seen contracts where this is ambiguous or tier-dependent.
Approved positioning
Customer owns all right, title, and interest in Outputs made available in the Customer's instance. Explicit in the Universal Amendment.

The definition of "Outputs" is scoped to outputs made available in the Customer's instance as part of the Service. This covers everything the customer sees and uses in Workday. Customers sometimes push to remove the "in the Customer's instance" limitation — Workday's position is to retain this because it is tied to the AI System definition and the ownership construct. Removing it would create ambiguity, not clarity.
When to escalate
If the customer wants to modify the definition of "Outputs," that is a Legal conversation. Do not agree to language changes in the moment.

Tier 2 — Less frequent, higher risk · Recognize early
"We don't want AI making employment decisions or other high-stakes calls automatically." ▼
What they're really worried about
This is a real legal concern, especially for HR buyers. EU AI Act and emerging employment law in multiple jurisdictions creates genuine liability for automated employment decisions. They want Workday to bear risk they see as Workday's responsibility.
Approved positioning
Workday positions AI as decision-support, not autonomous decision-making in high-risk contexts. This is a design principle, not a limitation.

The AUP expressly bars use of the Service for automated decision-making with legal effects — including employment decisions — unless a human makes, reviews, or oversees the final decision. The customer is responsible for the final decision based on Outputs. Frame this as Workday building AI responsibly. The human-in-the-loop requirement is the right architecture for high-stakes decisions.
When to escalate
If the customer wants to soften Disallowed Usage language around employment decisions, stop immediately. This is a hard line — escalate to Legal right away. Do not negotiate AUP terms on the fly.
Resource to send
Responsible AI FAQ · AI Fact Sheet (ethics and limitations section) · AUP (Disallowed Usage section)
"Why are we responsible for complying with the AUP? That's Workday's policy." ▼
What they're really worried about
The customer doesn't understand why they're being asked to own compliance obligations for an AI system they didn't build. They feel the AUP shifts risk onto them for Workday's technology decisions.
Approved positioning
The AUP flows down from Workday's own subprocessors and is standard across all enterprise SaaS. Workday must be able to suspend access if a takedown or legal requirement arises — the customer's acknowledgment of the AUP is what makes that legally possible. This is not Workday dumping risk; it is preserving the ability to protect the entire platform.

The AUP obligations mirror what the customer already has with AWS, Google, or Microsoft. Frame it as a standard operating agreement, not a unique burden.
When to escalate
If the customer wants to modify AUP obligations or carve out specific uses from their responsibilities, loop in Legal.

Tier 3 — Migration mechanics · Have a narrative ready
"We don't like dynamic terms / we want to renegotiate our legacy MSA / why do we have to migrate?" ▼
What they're really worried about
This cluster has multiple shapes: (a) genuine discomfort with URL-incorporated terms that can change mid-contract; (b) migration fatigue — they don't want to spend legal review time on a contract change; (c) opportunistic — using migration as a hook to reopen legacy MSA economics; (d) geographic/hosting commitments from their legacy MSA.
Approved positioning
Address the specific shape, not just the surface complaint.

On dynamic terms: Product Terms require 30-day notice and cannot materially decrease security, privacy, or SLA protections. Stronger than most SaaS contracts, where continued use equals acceptance of any change.

On migration fatigue: The terms are non-negotiable platform standards, which actually shortens legal review significantly. It's simpler than it looks — not a complex redline process.

On re-trade attempts: Migration is not a commercial reset. Pricing, SLAs, and protections carry forward. Be firm — if it escalates, involve your manager.

On "why migrate": Without migration, the customer cannot access new AI SKUs, Flex Credits, Sana, or next-generation innovations.
When to escalate
If the customer is attempting a material re-trade of legacy MSA risk allocation (liability caps, geographic commitments, indemnity), loop in Legal and your manager. Do not negotiate those terms independently.

Vendor watch-outs for sales conversations
Anthropic
  • No default SLA — "AS IS / AS AVAILABLE" for standard API
  • Consumer plans (Pro/Team) training ON by default as of Sept. 28, 2025 — shadow IT risk
  • Immediate suspension without notice for policy violations
  • No structured data export mechanism on termination
  • Bartz v. Anthropic ($1.5B settlement) did not release output infringement claims
  • Hallucination liability fully on the customer
Databricks
  • PayGo: Databricks can modify MCSA unilaterally — continued use = consent
  • Data loss explicitly excluded from consequential damages — even from Databricks negligence
  • 30-day data deletion after termination — no grace period, no read-only access
  • Apache Spark open-source IP carveout — the core compute engine is not covered by IP indemnity
  • 3 business days breach notification ≠ 72 calendar hours (GDPR gap)
GCP
  • AI/ML data use protections not explicit in base terms — require service-specific terms
  • Breach notification: "without undue delay" — no fixed clock
  • 12-month liability cap with no breach-specific enhancement in base contract
  • Multiple URL-incorporated documents add complexity for legal review
OpenAI
  • No default SLA — enterprise terms require sales negotiation
  • Breach notification: "without undue delay" — no contractual clock
  • 14-day pricing change notice only — other material changes can happen faster
  • No structured data export on termination
  • Copyright Shield (uncapped IP indemnity) applies to ChatGPT Enterprise only
Snowflake
  • Committed to 72-hour breach notification in DPA — took 45 days in the 2024 Neiman Marcus incident
  • URL-incorporated terms: changes can take effect with minimal notice
  • 2x liability cap requires a specific data protection breach trigger to activate
  • Customer-managed keys (Tri-Secret Secure) requires Business Critical tier
AWS
  • Continued use = acceptance of updated terms — no minimum notice for material changes
  • 12-month liability cap; no breach-specific enhancement in standard terms
  • No-training commitment is policy-based, not a standalone contractual obligation
  • SLA is per-service — requires knowing exactly which services are in scope
SAP
  • AI data use is opt-out policy via SAP for Me — not a hard contractual prohibition; enterprise customers pushed back publicly in 2024
  • AI output ownership: customer receives a license, not unencumbered IP ownership — outputs are subject to SAP's pre-existing rights in models and training data
  • RISE with SAP bundles create deep lock-in — infrastructure, software, and services under one SAP-managed contract; exit is complex
  • Renewal escalators of 5–7% annually compound quickly; credit conversion for on-prem customers decreases ~10% per year creating time-pressure
  • Licensing metrics (FUE, named users, digital documents) are complex — indirect access and API integrations can trigger unexpected true-up charges
Oracle
  • 2025 Oracle Health breach: Oracle communicated only by phone, asked hospitals to delay notifications, provided no written documentation — direct HIPAA exposure for customers
  • Hosting & Delivery Policies incorporated by reference and updatable unilaterally — Oracle determines what counts as "no material reduction"
  • HIPAA BAA requires a separate paid add-on license ("HIPAA Advanced Security for Fusion SaaS") — not automatic
  • AI output ownership: Oracle and its licensors retain all IP rights in AI Services; customers receive a license only
  • PO subordination trap: Oracle's ordering documents override any conflicting purchase order boilerplate — protective terms must be in the Oracle doc, not the PO
